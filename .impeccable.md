# jason-edelman.org — Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
Developers, civic technologists, potential collaborators, and anyone who has stumbled
in from one of the subprojects (abolish-lawns, power-explained, mithlond, ghent-zoning-reform).
Context: desktop browsing, probably sent a link. Low intent — they're evaluating whether
to read further. Need: orientation, character, and a clear sense of what this person builds.

### Brand Personality
Three words: **grounded, rigorous, provisional**

Not a portfolio site. Not a startup founder's landing page. The record of someone who
builds things seriously and thinks carefully about why. The commons framework runs
through everything — this should feel like it was made by the same person who wrote
the manifesto and the policy briefs, not a different person who runs a website.

### Aesthetic Direction
The physical object: field notes in a Moleskine with printed margin tabs. A 1970s
government technical report annotated in pencil by an anarchist.

- **Dark by default.** Already correct. Do not lighten.
- **Green accent** (`#5fa86e` / `#7ec9a0`) — ecosystem, commons, organic. Already correct.
- **Typography:** Georgia body is correct. Add Epilogue for display/headings — geometric,
  editorial, slightly condensed. Not a reflex font.
- **Density:** One column, tight, dense. No hero sections. No cards with shadows.
  No rounded rectangles. No gradients.
- **Anti-references:** Startup landing pages, portfolio sites with "hero" sections,
  generic tech blog aesthetics, anything with a navbar. This site has no navbar.

### Design Principles
1. **Restraint is the argument.** The site should feel like the work, not like marketing.
2. **Every element earns its place.** If a visual element doesn't carry information,
   it probably shouldn't be there.
3. **Subprojects are first-class.** They aren't portfolio items — they're the thing.
   The personal bio is context for the projects, not the other way around.
4. **Typography does the work.** No decorative elements. Hierarchy comes from
   font size, weight, and spacing — not color blocks, icons, or borders.
5. **The dev journal is as important as the project list.** It's where the actual
   intellectual work is visible.
